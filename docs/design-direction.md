## How I arrived here

The photo set does not read like a generic handyman portfolio. It repeatedly shows custom wood counters, wood tables, cabinetry, built-ins, fireplace/mantel work, barn doors, shop work, fences, decks, and bathroom/kitchen updates. The clearest differentiator is not “we do everything around the house.” It is that Mark’s work has a finish-carpentry and woodworking center of gravity.

The current images vary in polish. Some are older, soft, cropped, or clearly Facebook-era. That does not mean they are unusable. For a first draft, they are good enough to establish the hierarchy, tone, and project storytelling. Once Mark sees the site, the design should naturally prompt him to supply better versions of the same proof: finished room shots, before/during/after sets, close-ups of joinery and finish, and better shop/process photos.

## Positioning

Primary positioning:

> Custom home improvements built with third-generation craftsmanship.

Supporting idea:

> Miller Custom Works handles kitchens, built-ins, woodwork, finish details, and home improvement projects where the finished result matters.

The site should not try to sound luxurious, corporate, or trendy. It should sound plain-spoken and confident. The voice should feel like a skilled tradesman whose work is better than his marketing has ever been.

## What the site should not become

- Not a Kentucky-themed site.
- Not a flag-heavy patriotic design.
- Not a generic “licensed and insured handyman near me” template.
- Not a stock-photo remodeler site.
- Not a fake luxury interiors brand.
- Not a huge multi-page build before Mark confirms services, contact details, and best projects.

## Visual direction

### Mood

- Warm wood
- Shop-made
- Masculine but not dark for the sake of being dark
- Clean enough to feel current
- Rough enough to feel real
- Heritage as texture, not costume

### Palette

- Deep charcoal / near black for authority and contrast
- Warm cream for page background
- Walnut and amber tones pulled from the woodwork photos
- Muted brass/gold as a small accent
- Stone gray for fireplace/construction material references

No bright Kentucky blue. No state outline. No bourbon-bar cliché.

### Typography direction

Use a serif for the large display moments to create a craft/heritage feeling, paired with a straightforward sans-serif for body and navigation. The first draft uses system fonts so the repo stays dependency-free and fast. Later we can tune this with specific web fonts if desired.

### Image rules

Lead with detail, not generic room width. The strongest photos are close enough to show wood grain, finish, hardware, shelves, counters, or Mark working. Wide room photos are helpful, but only after the visitor already understands the craft.

Best first-draft image roles:

- Hero: wood counter / kitchen / craft close-up
- About/process: Mark working in the shop
- Project feature: custom wood counters and finished kitchen
- Supporting services: built-ins, fireplaces, bathrooms, decks/fences
- Heritage detail: flag display case and old card only as supporting proof

## Homepage strategy

### 1. Hero

The hero should immediately say this is custom craft, not a template contractor page. The CTA should push people to see the work or call/text.

### 2. Specialty lanes

Use three lanes:

- Custom Woodwork
- Kitchens & Built-ins
- Home Improvements

This gives range without sounding scattered.

### 3. Featured work

Show the strongest visual evidence early. Include a mix of counters, built-ins, fireplace, deck/fence, and bathroom work.

### 4. Craft/process section

Use Mark in the shop and wood detail shots. This is the credibility section. It explains why the work feels custom instead of assembled.

### 5. Project spotlight

Feature one project detail page even if copy is partly placeholder. This teaches the eventual site structure.

### 6. About Mark

Use third-generation craftsmanship carefully. It is a powerful line, but it should not become fake nostalgia. Keep it short and grounded.

### 7. Contact

For contractors, phone/text should be prominent. The first draft uses placeholder contact info until Mark confirms preferred lead path.

## Projects page strategy

The Projects page should not be a dump of all 70 photos. It should act like a curated proof wall with categories and future project-detail paths.

First-draft project groups:

- Custom wood counters and tables
- Kitchens and cabinetry
- Built-ins and fireplace work
- Bathrooms and vanities
- Exterior carpentry, decks, gates, and fences

## First project detail page

The first project detail page is “Custom Wood Counters & Kitchen Finish Work.” This is the best first detail page because the photo set has multiple related images that feel distinctive and handmade.

The page uses a case-study format:

- What was built
- Why it matters
- Details to notice
- Photo sequence
- CTA to discuss similar work

Some copy must remain draft/placeholder until Mark confirms project specifics.
